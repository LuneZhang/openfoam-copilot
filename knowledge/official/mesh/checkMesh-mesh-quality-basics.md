## Why this note exists

Mesh quality is one of the highest-leverage determinants of OpenFOAM robustness. Many apparent numerics or solver problems are actually mesh problems in disguise. `checkMesh` should be treated as an early diagnostic gate, not a ceremonial command run after the fact.

## What `checkMesh` is for

`checkMesh` inspects mesh topology and geometry quality. It does not prove a case will converge, but it quickly reveals whether the mesh has structural or geometric defects likely to destabilize discretization and pressure-velocity coupling.

Its role in an agent workflow is:

1. detect obvious mesh pathologies early
2. classify mesh-related risk before solver tuning
3. separate mesh-driven failures from dictionary-driven failures

## Key mesh-quality ideas

A mesh can be formally valid yet still numerically dangerous. Agents should care about both:

- **topological correctness**
- **numerical suitability**

Important quality concepts include:

- non-orthogonality
- skewness
- aspect ratio
- concavity
- cell determinant / inversion issues
- boundary topology consistency

## Why mesh quality matters numerically

Poor mesh quality can amplify:

- gradient error
- diffusion discretization error
- pressure correction instability
- residual oscillation
- sensitivity to aggressive convection schemes
- time-step restrictions in transient cases

The worse the mesh, the more conservative the numerics usually need to be.

## Practical reading frame for `checkMesh`

Do not read `checkMesh` as a pass/fail binary. Read it in three layers:

### 1. Fatal structural issues
These must be fixed before serious simulation.
Examples include:
- illegal topology
- degenerate/inverted cells
- missing/invalid boundary consistency

### 2. Severe quality warnings
These may not prevent startup, but often correlate with instability.
Examples include:
- very high non-orthogonality
- severe skewness
- badly shaped local cells near critical flow features

### 3. Manageable but important warnings
These do not always require remeshing, but they may require:
- more conservative numerics
- additional non-orthogonal correction
- smaller time steps
- stricter startup strategy

## Common high-risk quality symptoms

### High non-orthogonality
Often stresses diffusion handling and pressure correction. Cases with high non-orthogonality may require more conservative `laplacian` treatment and correction settings.

### High skewness
Often degrades transport accuracy and can interact badly with aggressive convection schemes.

### Poor cells near critical regions
A mesh can have acceptable global statistics yet still fail because the worst cells sit in:
- separation regions
- sharp gradients
- near-wall layers
- inlets/outlets
- interfaces or combustion zones

## Practical review checklist

Before trusting a run, verify:

1. `checkMesh` completes without fatal structural errors.
2. Quality warnings are identified and categorized by severity.
3. Worst cells are located, not just counted.
4. Critical physics regions are checked for local mesh weakness.
5. Numerics are chosen in awareness of mesh quality, not independently.
6. A bad mesh is not being masked by excessive solver damping.

## Common failure modes

- Ignoring severe `checkMesh` warnings because the solver still starts
- Blaming all divergence on turbulence or boundary conditions when poor cells are the main trigger
- Using aggressive convection schemes on a poor mesh
- Focusing on average mesh quality while worst-cell regions dominate failure behavior
- Treating mesh repair as optional after repeated floating point exceptions

## Anti-patterns

- Running `checkMesh` once and never reading the warning details
- Treating all mesh warnings as equally important
- Assuming a mesh generated by a standard workflow is automatically robust
- Trying to fix fundamentally bad mesh behavior only through relaxation factors

## Recommendation

- Run `checkMesh` before serious case debugging.
- Classify mesh issues into fatal / severe / manageable.
- Correlate worst-quality regions with expected high-gradient physics zones.
- If the mesh is poor, start with conservative numerics and reduced ambition.
- Escalate to remeshing or local mesh improvement when quality warnings line up with instability symptoms.

## Rationale

- Mesh quality directly shapes numerical stability and accuracy.
- Early mesh triage reduces wasted effort on false numerics fixes.
- A disciplined mesh-risk assessment gives agents a better debugging order.

## Applicability limits

- Acceptable thresholds depend on solver family, physics, discretization aggressiveness, and mesh strategy.
- A mesh with warnings can still produce useful results in some cases, but only with justified caution.
- `checkMesh` is necessary but not sufficient; some unstable meshes only reveal themselves under actual solution dynamics.
